Where Patience Becomes Art

Born and raised in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia — a city of monuments, iconic landmarks, and the rich cultural heritage of old Jeddah set against soaring modern architecture — Saba Haroon grew up surrounded by beauty in its most layered forms. That early impression never left her.

When she moved to Pakistan and it was time to choose a direction, the answer was clear: art. She pursued a Master's degree in Fine Arts from the University of Punjab (2007–2009) and a Master's in Interior Design from the National College of Arts (2010–2012), finding mentors along the way who pushed her to think beyond convention, challenge her creativity, and explore what it truly means to express emotion through form.

"I fold cranes as quiet extensions of myself. Each delicate fold holds time, patience, and a sense of presence."

— Saba Haroon

Her work draws from an ancient Japanese legend in which origami cranes symbolise wish fulfilment — tracing the journey from catastrophe to relief, from fracture to return. Hundreds of hand-folded cranes are arranged into sweeping formations: clouds, spirals, circles, and constellations that carry weight beyond their delicate paper form.

Guided by Wabi-Sabi, the Japanese philosophy of finding beauty in impermanence and imperfection, Saba's installations are meditative spaces. Light and shadow move across them. Individual cranes rest beside one another in quiet companionship. Among the red — the dominant hue of emotion and urgency — a few golden cranes appear, soft signals of something precious breaking through the collective.

Artist Statement

"Guided by Wabi-Sabi — the beauty of impermanence and imperfection — I fold cranes as quiet extensions of myself. Each delicate fold holds time, patience, and a sense of presence. As they gather, drift, or spiral, the cranes reflect changing emotions: stillness, longing, and return, existing in a quiet harmony. Their arrangements show moments of companionship and connection, where each crane rests beside another in a gentle pause. Light and shadow move across them, adding depth. Among them, a few red cranes appear as soft signs of emotion within the calm. In their fragile grouping, the self slowly disappears into repetition and gesture, creating a meditative space where beauty lives in what is temporary, imperfect, and softly unfinished."
